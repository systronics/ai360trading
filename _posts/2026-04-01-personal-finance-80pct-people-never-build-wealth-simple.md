## The Setup
As we observe the financial struggles of many individuals, it's clear that around 80% of people never build wealth, and this statistic is alarming. The primary reason behind this is the lack of a well-structured financial plan. From my own trading experience, I've seen how a simple yet effective approach can make a significant difference. The 50-30-20 rule, for instance, is a great starting point. It suggests allocating 50% of your income towards necessary expenses, 30% towards discretionary spending, and 20% towards saving and debt repayment. Let's consider an example: if you earn $50,000 per year in the US, you would allocate $25,000 towards necessary expenses, $15,000 towards discretionary spending, and $10,000 towards saving and debt repayment.

## What the Data Actually Says
The data indicates that people who follow a disciplined financial plan tend to achieve their long-term goals. For instance, having an emergency fund in place can provide a sense of security and stability. The general rule of thumb is to have 3-6 months' worth of expenses saved in an easily accessible savings account. In the UK, for example, if your monthly expenses are £2,000, you should aim to save between £6,000 and £12,000 in your emergency fund. Similarly, in Brazil, if your monthly expenses are R$5,000, you should aim to save between R$15,000 and R$30,000. In India, if your monthly expenses are ₹50,000, you should aim to save between ₹1,50,000 and ₹3,00,000.

## How This Affects Each Country
The concept of building wealth is universal, but the approach may vary depending on the country and its economic conditions. In the US, for instance, the 401(k) plan is a popular retirement savings option. In the UK, it's the ISA (Individual Savings Account). In Brazil, it's the FGTS (Fundo de Garantia do Tempo de Serviço). And in India, it's the NPS (National Pension System) or the EPF (Employees' Provident Fund). For example, if you're a working professional in the US, you can contribute up to $19,500 to your 401(k) plan in 2026. In the UK, you can contribute up to £20,000 to your ISA. In Brazil, you can contribute up to R$7,913 to your FGTS. And in India, you can contribute up to ₹1,50,000 to your NPS or EPF.

## The Risk Nobody's Talking About
One of the significant risks that people often overlook is debt. High-interest debt, in particular, can be a major obstacle to building wealth. For instance, if you have a credit card debt of $2,000 with an interest rate of 20%, you can expect to pay around $400 in interest alone over the course of a year. In the UK, if you have a credit card debt of £2,000 with an interest rate of 20%, you can expect to pay around £400 in interest. In Brazil, if you have a credit card debt of R$10,000 with an interest rate of 20%, you can expect to pay around R$2,000 in interest. And in India, if you have a credit card debt of ₹2,00,000 with an interest rate of 20%, you can expect to pay around ₹40,000 in interest. To get out of debt, it's essential to create a debt repayment plan and stick to it. You can start by prioritizing your debts, negotiating with your creditors, and making regular payments.
